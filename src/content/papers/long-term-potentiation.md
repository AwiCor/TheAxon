In 1949 the psychologist Donald Hebb proposed a rule so compact it now reads
like a slogan: neurons that fire together, wire together. Long-term potentiation,
or LTP, is the closest thing neuroscience has found to that rule made flesh.

## A gate that counts coincidences

The story centres on the synapse — the junction where one neuron passes a signal
to the next — and on a particular receptor for the neurotransmitter glutamate:
the **NMDA receptor**. This receptor is a coincidence detector. It opens only
when two things happen at once: the sending neuron releases glutamate, *and* the
receiving neuron is already depolarised from its own recent activity.

That double condition is the whole trick. A magnesium ion normally plugs the
channel; only when the receiving cell is sufficiently excited does the plug pop
free, letting calcium flood in. Calcium is the signal that says: *these two cells
were active together — record it.*

## From a pulse to a lasting change

The calcium influx sets off a cascade. Enzymes such as CaMKII are activated, and
they drive more **AMPA receptors** into the receiving membrane. With more
receptors listening, the same amount of glutamate now produces a larger
response. The synapse has been potentiated — strengthened — and it stays that way.

Sustained potentiation recruits a second phase. New proteins are synthesised,
and the synapse is physically remodelled: the contact grows, sometimes splitting
into two. What began as a fleeting electrical coincidence has become a change in
structure.

> Early LTP adjusts the sensitivity of an existing connection. Late LTP rebuilds
> the connection itself. The transition from one to the other is, in miniature,
> the transition from holding something in mind to remembering it.

## The limits of the metaphor

It is tempting to call LTP "the memory molecule," but the honest claim is
narrower. LTP is a mechanism by which synaptic strength can be durably changed,
demonstrated most cleanly in the hippocampus, a structure essential to forming
new memories. Whether a given memory is stored as a particular pattern of
potentiated synapses remains a question at the frontier.

What LTP does give us is a proof of principle: experience can leave a lasting,
molecular trace at the exact junctions where neurons talk. The brain does not
merely process information. It rewrites itself in response to it.
