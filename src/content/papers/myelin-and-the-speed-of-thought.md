A bare axon is a poor cable. Its membrane leaks charge, and the signal it
carries — a wave of ions crossing that membrane — weakens as it travels. Left
to physics alone, a thought would dissipate before it crossed the brain. Myelin
is the evolutionary fix.

## An insulator with gaps

Myelin is a fatty sheath wrapped in tight spirals around the axon by supporting
cells: oligodendrocytes in the brain and spinal cord, Schwann cells in the
periphery. Each cell insulates a short segment, and between the segments sit
bare patches called the **nodes of Ranvier**. The arrangement matters as much as
the material. The insulated stretches stop the current from leaking outward; the
nodes, densely packed with voltage-gated sodium channels, give the signal a
place to regenerate.

The result is **saltatory conduction** — from the Latin *saltare*, to leap. The
action potential does not crawl smoothly down the fibre. It jumps from node to
node, regenerating at full strength each time it lands.

> Insulation alone would only delay the inevitable decay. It is the rhythm of
> insulation and interruption — sheath, node, sheath, node — that lets the
> signal renew itself as it goes.

## The payoff in numbers

The speed-up is dramatic. Conduction velocity in an unmyelinated axon is often
around one metre per second. Wrap that same fibre in myelin and the signal can
travel at a hundred metres per second or more. A myelinated nerve also spends
far less energy, because the ion pumps that reset the membrane only have to work
hard at the nodes rather than along the entire length.

Two design variables set the final speed:

- **Axon diameter** — wider fibres conduct faster, myelinated or not.
- **Internode length** — the spacing of the nodes, tuned so the leaping signal
  arrives at each node still strong enough to fire the next.

## When the insulation fails

Because so much depends on the sheath, its loss is catastrophic in a specific,
legible way. In demyelinating diseases such as multiple sclerosis, the immune
system strips myelin from central axons. The exposed fibre leaks current again;
saltatory conduction breaks down; signals slow, scatter, or fail outright. The
symptoms — blurred vision, numbness, weakness — are the downstream reading of a
timing system thrown out of step.

Understanding myelin, then, is not only a matter of anatomy. It is a way of
seeing the brain as a network that solved a hard engineering problem, and of
recognising how narrow the margin is that keeps it solved.
