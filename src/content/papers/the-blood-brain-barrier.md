Every other organ tolerates a certain traffic between blood and tissue. The
brain does not. Its neurons operate on electrical and chemical margins so fine
that an ordinary fluctuation in blood chemistry — a spike in potassium after a
meal, a passing toxin — could scramble their signalling. The blood–brain barrier
is the structure that buys the brain its stable internal weather.

## Not a wall but a filter

The barrier is not a sheet of concrete; it is the wall of the brain's own
capillaries, made unusually tight. Three features set these vessels apart:

- **Tight junctions.** The cells lining brain capillaries are stitched together
  by protein seals that close the gaps found in ordinary vessels, so nothing
  slips *between* the cells.
- **Selective transporters.** What the brain needs — glucose, certain amino
  acids — is carried *through* the cells by dedicated protein pumps.
- **A supporting cast.** Astrocytes wrap the vessels with their end-feet and
  signal the lining cells to maintain their tightness.

The effect is a gate that reads what tries to cross it. Small, fat-soluble
molecules — oxygen, carbon dioxide, alcohol, anaesthetics — pass freely. Almost
everything else needs a ticket.

> The barrier's genius is not that it blocks. Anything can block. Its genius is
> that it blocks selectively, admitting fuel while excluding disorder.

## The cost of a good defence

A barrier this effective is also a barrier to medicine. The great majority of
drugs — nearly all large-molecule therapies, most antibiotics, most cancer
agents — cannot cross it. A treatment that works everywhere else in the body may
be useless against a brain tumour or a central infection simply because it never
reaches its target.

Researchers pursue several ways around the problem: hijacking the barrier's own
transporters to smuggle drugs across, opening the junctions transiently with
focused ultrasound, or delivering agents directly into the cerebrospinal fluid.
Each strategy is really a negotiation with the same structure evolution built to
keep such things out.

## A barrier that can break

The barrier is not infallible, and its failure is itself a disease process. In
stroke, infection, and traumatic injury the junctions loosen, the brain's
protected chemistry is breached, and swelling follows. Studying how the barrier
breaks down has become as important as studying how it holds — because in both
directions, the fate of the brain is written at the capillary wall.
