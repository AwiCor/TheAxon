The body's response to threat is often described in the language of feeling —
alarm, tension, dread. Underneath the feeling is a circuit as orderly as any in
physiology: a three-step relay that begins in the brain and ends in the adrenal
glands, releasing cortisol in an amount matched to the demand.

## Three organs, one signal

The axis is named for its stations. The **hypothalamus**, sensing a stressor,
releases corticotropin-releasing hormone (CRH). CRH travels a short distance to
the **pituitary**, which responds by secreting adrenocorticotropic hormone
(ACTH) into the bloodstream. ACTH reaches the **adrenal glands**, perched atop
the kidneys, and prompts their outer layer to release **cortisol**.

Cortisol is the axis's output and its message to the body: mobilise. It raises
blood glucose, sharpens attention, and temporarily quiets systems — digestion,
immunity, reproduction — that can wait until the threat has passed. In short
bursts this is adaptive. It is the biochemistry of rising to an occasion.

## The off-switch

What makes the axis a system rather than a fire alarm is that it regulates
itself. Cortisol, once high, feeds back on the hypothalamus and pituitary and
tells them to stop. CRH and ACTH fall; cortisol production winds down; the
system returns to baseline.

> A stress response that could not end would be as dangerous as one that could
> not begin. The feedback loop is what turns a surge into a pulse.

This negative feedback is the same logic a thermostat uses. The set-point is not
fixed, though — it follows a daily rhythm, cortisol peaking in the morning and
ebbing at night, so the axis is always tuned to the expected demands of the hour.

## When feedback fails

Chronic stress is, in endocrine terms, a feedback problem. When the axis is
driven hard for weeks or months, the receptors that sense cortisol can grow less
responsive, and the off-switch loses its grip. Cortisol stays elevated. The
short-term trade-offs that once helped — suppressed immunity, raised blood
sugar, borrowed energy — become standing liabilities, and the same hormone that
helped the body rise to a threat begins to wear it down.

Two clinical extremes bracket the normal range: Cushing's syndrome, where
cortisol is pathologically high, and Addison's disease, where the adrenal output
fails. Between them lies the ordinary human experience of stress — the same axis,
the same hormones, read at a different volume.
