The body keeps blood glucose within a remarkably tight range — roughly four to
seven millimoles per litre — despite meals that flood the blood with sugar and
fasts that drain it. It does so with a pair of opposing hormones from the
pancreas: **insulin**, which lowers glucose, and **glucagon**, which raises it.

The full paper is set as a downloadable PDF below. It follows the two hormones
through a single day of eating and fasting, treats their balance as a classic
negative-feedback loop, and reframes both types of diabetes as distinct failures
of that loop.
